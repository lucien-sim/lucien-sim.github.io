About me

I have been fascinated by the weather since I was a child. This fascination brought me to Penn State, where I received a BS and MS in Meteorology and Atmospheric Science in August 2018.

During my education, I developed a strong analytical background through work on my MS thesis, research at the NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies, research at the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Earth System Research Laboratory, and through field-based courses I took in the high Arctic. In these experiences, I evaluated climate models, analyzed large and diverse observational datasets, and learned how to use numerical models to study atmospheric processes. I also developed a strong background in scientific communication to both technical and non-technical audiences.

I am currently building a background in catastrophe risk, statistical modeling, and machine learning through independent study and personal projects. Links to these projects are provided below.

Research

Penn State University

  • OBJECTIVE: Learn about how radaitive cooling drives the formation of Arctic stratocumulus clouds in the Arctic.
  • SO WHAT? These types of clouds play a huge role in the Arctic climate, and could be contributing to rapid Arctic climate change. Scientists cannot understand this contribution until they understand how Arctic stratocumlus clouds form.
  • APPROACH: I used the Rapid Radiative Transfer Model and observations from the North Slope of Alaska to determine that Arctic stratocumulus clouds can form by radiative cooling. I then used over 2000 single column model simulations to examine the formation process and its sensitivities. From the simulations, I found that the sensitivities are strong and might help explain a key characteristic of the Arctic climate
  • OUTCOME: Article submitted to the Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres (December 2018).
  • SKILLS GAINED: Completed project with limited supervision. Learned Fortran so that I could understand, debug, and add to the poorly-documented model. Used MATLAB for processing and analysis of 110 GB of observations and model output.

NOAA Earth System Research Laboratory

  • OBJECTIVE: Statistically characterize the thermodynamic structure of Arctic stratocumulus clouds.
  • SO WHAT? Arctic stratocumulus clouds are important to the Arctic climate, and an understanding of the clouds' thermodynamic structure helps us understand why they persist.
  • APPROACH: Used MATLAB to derive summary statistics from ten years of weather baloon, radar, lidar, and microwave radiometer observations from the North Slope of Alaska and Summit Station, Greenland.
  • OUTCOME: Oral presentations at the Hollings Scholarship research symposium, and at the 2017 annual meeting of the American Meteorological Society.
  • SKILLS GAINED: Experimental design and execution. Drew insight from a large quantity (>500 GB) of messy data from disparate sources. Presentation of scientific concepts to technical and non-technical audiences.

NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies

  • OBJECTIVE: Evaluate precipitation forecasts by a regional climate model over West Africa.
  • SO WHAT? My mentor was developing the model for the African Center of Meteorological Application for Development (ACMAD). ACMAD aims to provide weather and climate information to promote sustainable development in Africa, and providing accurate precipitation forecasts is a crucial aspect of their mission.
  • APPROACH: Wrote Python scripts to compare model precipitation forecasts with observations from weather stations in West Africa, and with model and satellite-derived datasets. Worked with a NYC high school student and science teacher through the entire research process.
  • OUTCOME: Oral presentation at NASA GISS, poster presentation at the CUNY Summer STEM Research Symposium. Summarized results in a research report.
  • SKILLS GAINED: Became effective in communicating scientific concepts to non-technical audiences--I gave countless lessons in meteorology and Python to the high school student and science teacher, who had no background in meteorology or scientific data analysis.

University Centre in Svalbard

I spent Spring 2016 taking courses in Air-Sea-Ice Interaction and Snow and Ice Processes at the University Centre in Svalbard on the Svalbard archipelago. In both courses, end-of-semester projects involved using data I collected from an icebreaker and nearby glacier to answer a science question. I was responsible for planning my study, and collecting, processing, and analyzing all the data I needed to carry out the study.

SKILLS GAINED: Design and execution of scientific studies. Data collection, processing, and analysis using Python and MATLAB. Collaboration: data collection was a collaborative effort in a harsh and dangerous environment. I learned how important patience, flexibility, communication, and understanding are while working with others under physically and emotionally difficult circumstances.
